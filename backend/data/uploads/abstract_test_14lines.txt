This study explores the application of the TextRank algorithm for automatic text summarization in a backend service environment. 
TextRank is a graph-based ranking model that identifies the most important sentences in a document using structural relationships rather than supervised learning. 
In this work, sentences are represented as nodes in a graph, while edges are constructed using cosine similarity derived from TF-IDF vectorization. 
The resulting similarity matrix is used to build a weighted graph, enabling the application of the PageRank algorithm to compute sentence importance scores. 
To improve summary quality, additional heuristics such as position bias and sentence length normalization are incorporated. 
These adjustments aim to prioritize informative sentences that typically appear earlier in structured texts such as academic papers. 
A redundancy filtering mechanism is also introduced to ensure diversity in the selected sentences by removing highly similar candidates. 
The system is designed as a stateless service, making it suitable for integration into a FastAPI-based backend. 
Special attention is given to thread safety by avoiding shared mutable objects during vectorization. 
Performance considerations such as limiting maximum sentence count and optimizing similarity thresholds are included to ensure scalability. 
The service demonstrates strong performance in extracting meaningful summaries from medium-length documents. 
Experimental observations show that combining TF-IDF similarity with graph-based ranking produces coherent and relevant summaries without the need for labeled data. 
The modular design allows for easy extension, including the integration of embedding-based similarity methods. 
Overall, the approach provides a practical and efficient solution for unsupervised text summarization in real-world applications. 
Future work may focus on improving semantic understanding through deep learning-based representations.
